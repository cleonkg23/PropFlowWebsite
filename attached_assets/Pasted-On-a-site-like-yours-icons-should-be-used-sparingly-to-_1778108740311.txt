On a site like yours, icons should be used sparingly to reinforce meaning and help users scan the page without overwhelming the minimalist aesthetic. Simple, thin‑line icons with rounded corners work well alongside the serif typeface and muted palette. Here are some suggestions:

Primary feature icons: Each of the three key pain‑points (“Repairs and tenant messages,” “Viewings and document chasing,” “Landlord and contractor updates”) would benefit from a small line icon preceding the heading—a wrench or toolbox for repairs, a calendar with a key or door for viewings, and a house with a speech bubble for landlord updates. Set these icons in your new accent color (burnt orange) so they stand out and draw the eye to each column’s title.
Process and steps: In the “How it works” section, consider adding subtle icons or small illustrations above the numbered steps—e.g. a jigsaw piece for “Pick one messy workflow,” a map marker for “Map how it works today,” a cog for “Configure the board,” a play button for “Run it on real work,” and a plus sign for “Add more workflows.” Keeping these icons in a softer tone of your accent colour or a muted teal helps distinguish the steps without distracting from the text.
Testimonials and metrics: For the “38 s” conversion card, a stopwatch icon in dark green or burnt orange can convey the time‑saving benefit at a glance. For customer quotes, you might place a small quotation‑mark icon or a speech bubble in green above the text.
Interactive cues: Where you have expandable content (such as the plus signs that reveal details in the integration section), make the plus icons slightly larger and use the accent colour to signal interactivity. A small right‑arrow or chevron in the same colour could also help users understand they can click to expand.
Contact details: At the bottom of the page, use universally recognised icons—a paper plane for email and the LinkedIn logo—for your contact links. Colour these icons with your primary dark green or, if you want them to stand out more, the accent orange. This maintains consistency and makes your calls to action clearer.

Stick to one accent colour for all primary icons (burnt orange, as discussed) and reserve muted green or a soft teal for secondary elements. This way, icons complement the content and guide users’ attention without conflicting with the site’s calm and professional style.
